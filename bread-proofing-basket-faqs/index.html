Bread proofing basket questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A bread proofing basket (banneton) helps dough hold its shape and develop a crisp crust by wicking moisture. Look for unlined rattan or wood pulp for artisan loaves, and consider size and shape based on your recipe. The key is proper care: never wash it with soap, just brush and air dry.

What is a bread proofing basket and why do I need one?

A proofing basket, also called a banneton, is a container that supports dough during its final rise. It is typically made from natural materials like rattan or wood pulp, often with a linen liner. The basket&#8217;s purpose is twofold: it keeps the loaf in a nice round or oval shape, and the porous surface wicks away moisture from the dough&#8217;s exterior. That drying effect helps create a crisp, bakery-style crust when the bread hits a hot oven. You do not strictly need one to bake bread, but if you want tall, well-shaped loaves with an open crumb and that artisan look, a proofing basket makes a real difference. It also frees up counter space because you can proof the dough in the basket and flip it directly onto a baking stone or Dutch oven.

Rattan vs. wood pulp: which material is better?

Rattan bannetons are the traditional choice. They are lightweight, very breathable, and develop a natural non-stick patina over time. Rattan loops leave the classic spiral marks on the crust, which many home bakers love. The main downside is durability: rattan can split or break if dropped or soaked in water. Wood pulp (sometimes called cane) bannetons are denser and more rigid. They are less likely to crack and often cost a bit less. They still wick moisture well, but they do not leave as distinct a pattern. For beginners, wood pulp can be a little more forgiving. Both materials work beautifully; choose rattan if you want that iconic look and don&#8217;t mind being gentle, or wood pulp for a sturdier basket that still does the job.

What size proofing basket should I buy?

Size matters more than you might think. Most baskets are sold by diameter, but the useful measurement is how much flour the basket can hold. A small basket (roughly 8 to 9 inches across) works well for loaves made with 500 grams of flour. A medium basket (about 10 inches) handles 600 to 700 grams of flour. Large baskets (11 inches or more) suit 800 to 1000 gram recipes. If you bake a standard country loaf with 500 to 600 grams of flour, a 9- or 10-inch round basket is a safe start. Oval baskets are sized similarly by length along the longest axis. When in doubt, go slightly larger than you think you need: a bigger basket can accommodate a relaxed proof, while an undersized basket will force the dough to spill over and may cause sticking.

Round vs. oval bannetons: which shape should I choose?

Shape determines the final look of your loaf. Round bannetons produce boules (round loaves), which are classic and fit beautifully in a Dutch oven. Oval baskets give you batards, the elongated shape often seen on bakery shelves. Batards slice into more consistently sized sandwich pieces, while boules are great for rustic tables. If you bake a variety of breads, many bakers own one of each. But if you only want to start with one, consider your typical bake vessel. If you use a round Dutch oven, a round basket is the natural match. If you bake freeform on a stone or steel, either shape works fine. Just match the basket shape to the shape you want your finished loaf to have.

How do I season and maintain a new proofing basket?

New baskets, especially rattan, need a little preparation before first use. Brush off any loose dust, then lightly mist the basket with water and let it dry completely. This &#8220;seasoning&#8221; step helps the fibers swell and reduces the chance of sticking. After the basket is dry, dust it generously with rice flour or a blend of rice and all-purpose flour. After each bake, turn the basket upside down and knock out excess flour. Use a stiff brush to remove dried dough bits. Never submerge the basket in water or use soap; moisture and detergent ruin the natural fibers. If the basket smells sour, let it sun-dry for a few hours. Once properly cared for, a banneton can last for years and actually gets better with use as the patina builds.

Can I use a proofing basket with high-hydration dough?

Yes, but it takes a bit more technique. Very wet doughs (70% hydration and above) can be sticky and prone to slumping if the basket is not well-floured. Use a heavy dusting of rice flour, which is coarser and less absorbent than wheat flour, to create a barrier. Some bakers also line the basket with a linen liner, which helps handle sticky doughs and makes cleanup easier. Even with a liner, do not expect your basket to look pristine after a high-hydration proof. Flour will form a crust on the basket surface, and that is fine. The key is to let the dough tighten up with a bench rest before placing it in the basket, and to be gentle when turning it out. With practice, high-hydration loaves come out beautifully from a well-seasoned rattan basket.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need a linen liner for my proofing basket?

Not strictly, but a liner makes handling sticky or high-hydration doughs much easier. Linen liners also simplify cleanup because you can just shake out the liner after use. Many bakers skip the liner with well-floured rattan and prefer the spiral marks the bare basket leaves.

Can I proof dough overnight in a banneton?

Yes, in fact overnight cold proofing in the refrigerator is a common technique. The basket allows the dough to hold its shape while the cold slows fermentation for better flavor. Just cover the basket loosely with a plastic bag or a shower cap to prevent the dough from drying out.

Why does my dough stick to the banneton?

Sticking usually happens when the basket is not floured heavily enough or when the dough is too wet for the flour you used. Switch to rice flour, which is less absorbent, and make sure you dust the basket generously between uses. Also, make sure your dough is not over-proofed: if it feels very slack, it will stick.

What is the best flour to dust a proofing basket?

Rice flour is the gold standard because it does not absorb moisture like wheat flour, so it stays dry and creates a non-stick layer. A 50/50 blend of rice flour and all-purpose flour works well too. Some bakers use semolina or rye flour for extra texture on the crust, but those will stick more easily.

Can I wash my proofing basket in the dishwasher?

Absolutely not. Dishwasher detergent and hot water will destroy the natural fibers and warp the basket. Always hand-brush your banneton and let it air dry completely. If you need to refresh a smelly basket, wipe it with a damp cloth (no soap) and dry it in direct sunlight.

Do I need to oil or treat a new banneton?

No oil needed. Just mist it lightly with water to season the fibers, let it dry, then dust with flour. Over time the basket will develop a natural patina from the flour and dough that actually improves non-stick properties. Never apply cooking spray or oil, as it will go rancid and ruin the basket.

In shortA good proofing basket is one of the few tools that truly elevates home bread baking without costing a fortune. Focus on material and size that match your typical loaf, maintain it with a brush and a dry cloth, and you will get years of beautiful, crusty bread. Remember: the basket is a helper, not a magic wand&#8212;great dough and careful shaping still do the real work.
